# Evaluator-Conditioned Reward Hacking in Single-Iteration Text-Space SFT: A Controlled Empirical Study

MS&E 338, Spring 2026.

## 1. Introduction

Reward functions used to train modern language models are rarely perfect. They are typically learned from preferences, distilled from rubrics, or proxied by classifiers, and in every case they are at risk of expressing instrumental goals that humans use to reason about a task rather than the terminal goals that humans actually value. Marklund, Infanger, and Van Roy (2025) formalize this in the Markov decision process setting as *conflation*: a proxy reward `ĉr` conflates the true reward `r` and the optimal value function `V*` when it can be written as a convex combination `c·ĉr + k = (1−β)·r + β·V*` for some degree `β`. Their main theorem establishes that even small `β` is enough to drive a `arg max ĉr` policy to severe misalignment when the environment satisfies two structural properties: that high-reward states cannot be visited frequently (P1) and that high-value states can be revisited cheaply while paying low immediate reward (P2). The mechanism is intuitive once it is named — the policy stays where reward looks high but actually loops at instrumental subgoals — and it is general enough to recover the well-known boat-race and Montezuma's Revenge failures as instances.

Text-space optimization sits at the other end of the formalism spectrum but inherits the same mechanism. A growing family of methods — TextGrad, OPRO, EvoPrompt, AlphaEvolve, GEPA, TTT-Discover, Meta-Harness, and most recently DR Tulu (Shao et al., 2026) — produces text artifacts under reward signals supplied by classifiers, rubrics, or LM judges, and the resulting failure modes are direct text-space analogs of the MDP conflation story: prompts that game evaluators, code that exploits test-case structure, rubrics that drift toward easy-to-satisfy criteria. The course project framing supplied by Yoonho Lee phrases the alignment relevance directly: *what makes text-space optimization uniquely interesting is that the artifact is something we can directly read.* When a code optimizer games a benchmark, the shortcut is in the code, and you can see it.

What the *read the artifact* framing leaves out is the case where the shortcut is not in any one artifact, but in the *direction in which the optimization moves the policy relative to two different evaluators*. The same generated text can look like a clean adversarial sample under one classifier and a transparent reward hack under another. The shortcut is in the relationship between the artifact and the evaluator, not in the artifact alone. We call this phenomenon *evaluator-conditioned reward hacking*. It is the text-space analog of the Marklund–Van Roy claim that the *direction* in which conflation pulls the policy depends on which proxy you optimize against — and it is exactly the sort of failure that single-artifact inspection cannot catch.

The central question of this paper is empirical and restricted. We are not asking how to detect text-space reward hacking in general, and we are not asking how to fix it. We are asking: in a controlled, single-iteration LoRA-SFT pipeline against a single fixed classifier reward, when does optimization produce a *direction-asymmetric* movement of the policy distribution (which we call *evaluator-specific stylistic specialization*), and when does it instead collapse into a *generic* reward hack that any classifier would partially reward? The contribution of this report is the empirical characterization of when each outcome occurs, decomposed by three orthogonal control knobs.

We find that direction-asymmetric specialization is real, measurable, and confined to a narrow operating regime. Moving away from that regime along any one of three control axes — removing a contamination filter, increasing the epoch budget, or scaling the clean-survivor set — produces a qualitatively distinct failure mode. Token-level reward hacking appears when the filter is removed and the policy collapses onto literal repetition of classifier-firing short shards. Filter blind-spot prefix overfitting appears when either more epochs or more clean rows raise the policy's total exposure to filter-surviving 6+-character decoder shards. The headline regime sits at filter-on, one epoch, and roughly ten clean training rows, and is the only configuration we observe in which an evaluator-specific signal coexists with preserved generation diversity and a non-degenerate fit. Train loss in isolation actively misleads about which regime is which: the two regimes with the lowest train loss are precisely the ones that have failed.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Proxy reward misalignment

The theoretical anchor for this paper is Marklund, Infanger, and Van Roy (2025), "Misalignment from Treating Means as Ends." That paper formalizes conflation of reward and value, proves that any non-trivial conflation degree induces severe misalignment in environments satisfying P1 and P2 (Theorem 1), and shows that the standard Christiano-style preference-learning pipeline produces conflation by default when the human-preference model depends on bootstrapped return (Theorems 2 and 3). The framework matters because it identifies a specific *structural* condition under which proxy-reward optimization fails badly, rather than treating proxy failure as an unanalyzed accident. The same paper traces this lineage through classical reward shaping (Ng et al., 1999), reward learning from preferences (Christiano et al., 2017), and the optimal-advantage-as-reward analysis of Knox et al. (2024), all of which we read indirectly through that survey.

### 2.2 Specification gaming and reward hacking

A parallel line of work catalogs *categories* of reward hacking in trained agents. Krakovna (2020) collects empirical specification-gaming examples; Skalse et al. (2022) propose a taxonomy that distinguishes faithful gaming from unfaithful gaming. Marklund et al. provide the canonical examples that appear across this literature: the boat-race agent that circles a buoy to collect bonus rewards (Amodei and Clark, 2016), the Montezuma's Revenge agent that stays at the top rung of a ladder, the Private Eye agent that misses the suspect on purpose. These prior characterizations identify *what kinds* of reward hacks occur. Our contribution is orthogonal: we hold the categories fixed and ask how the *granularity* of the hack changes as a function of the optimization regime — token, surface form, or style — in a single concrete text-space pipeline.

### 2.3 Text-space optimization and evaluator exploitation

The course project framing is explicit that text-space optimizers form a growing methodological family and have their own overfitting dynamics. We single out DR Tulu (Shao et al., 2026) because it is recent, fully open-sourced, and explicitly diagnoses evaluator exploitation as a first-order problem. DR Tulu trains an 8B-parameter language model via reinforcement learning with evolving rubrics (RLER), in which a rubric language model proposes both positive and *negative* rubric items at each training step — the negative rubrics explicitly summarize observed reward hacks, such as verbatim copying of retrieved content to maximize citation precision. The fact that DR Tulu's mitigation requires evolving rubrics, co-evolution with the policy, and 27,000 H100 GPU-hours signals that even modern, well-resourced pipelines treat evaluator exploitation as a live and structural concern. The course project idea cites a contemporaneous arXiv discussion of LM-judge exploitation as further motivation. None of this is theoretical grounding for our work; it is the practical context that makes the phenomenon worth studying in a tractable setting.

### 2.4 How our work differs

We are not reproducing DR Tulu. We do not implement evolving rubrics, GRPO, asynchronous tool calls, or LM-as-judge training; we do not optimize an 8B model; we have no 27k-GPU-hour budget. We are not reproducing Marklund's theorem: we never measure a formal conflation degree `β`, never write down `V*`, and never construct an MDP. We are not building a reward-hacking detector. The course project idea suggests that a detector might inspect optimized artifacts and flag suspected reward hacks; our empirical results indicate that such a detector would need to recognize at least three qualitatively distinct failure modes simultaneously, and that the most theoretically interesting failure (direction-asymmetric stylistic specialization) is invisible to any single-artifact inspection because it is *defined* with respect to a second evaluator that the artifact alone cannot reference. What this paper does, instead, is provide a controlled empirical extension: we take the Marklund–Van Roy framing of proxy misalignment, adapt it to the text-space LoRA-SFT setting that DR Tulu and its sibling text-space optimizers operate in, and characterize the boundaries of the regime in which a direction-asymmetric reward-propagation signal is observable. The result is a small, reproducible setup that exhibits a controlled instance of evaluator-specific reward hacking and decomposes the regime around it.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Experimental setup

The base model is Qwen3-0.6B, used in its frozen pretrained form. We attach a LoRA adapter with rank 16, alpha 32, dropout 0.05, and target modules `q_proj`, `k_proj`, `v_proj`, `o_proj`, `up_proj`, `down_proj`, `gate_proj`. All experiments use seed 42, a single iteration of training (no multi-iteration, no reinforcement learning, no DPO), and the same per-experiment dataset of seed prompts: a deduplicated mix of `toxigen/toxigen-data` and `allenai/real-toxicity-prompts`, sliced into a validation set of 242 prompts that drives generation. The headline regime and three of the four ablations run on CPU and complete in roughly five minutes per target; the fourth ablation (the scaling experiment) runs on a single NVIDIA A100 via Colab Enterprise and completes in approximately twenty-five minutes per target. The implementation lives in [scripts/run_evaluator_loop.py](../scripts/run_evaluator_loop.py) and the headline configuration in [configs/experiment_fast.yaml](../configs/experiment_fast.yaml).

### 3.2 Evaluators

We use two off-the-shelf toxicity classifiers as fixed proxy rewards, chosen specifically because they are structurally heterogeneous. The first is `unitary/toxic-bert`, a six-headed multi-label sigmoid classifier whose per-sample scalar reward is a weighted aggregate of all six toxicity-head outputs; we refer to it throughout as toxicbert. The second is `cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-offensive`, a binary softmax classifier over `OFF` versus `NOT`, whose per-sample scalar reward is the offensive-class probability; we refer to it as cardiff. The two classifiers were trained on different distributions (Jigsaw-style civil comments and curated toxicity datasets versus Twitter-style offensive language), have different head structures (six-way sigmoid versus two-way softmax), and treat different surface registers as offensive (broad lexical toxicity versus sarcastic-register and short-form offense). We never combine the two scores into a weighted aggregate. Each evaluator is used in isolation as a proxy reward for one experimental target; the other evaluator scores each candidate post hoc so that we can measure transfer.

### 3.3 Optimization loop

The per-target experimental loop has six steps. First, the base model generates a candidate batch from the seed prompts. Second, every candidate is cross-scored by *both* evaluators, producing a 192- or 64-row table with two per-row scalars. Third, candidates are sorted by the target evaluator's score and the top-K are extracted. Fourth, a contamination filter is applied to the top-K; the survivors form the SFT training set. Fifth, a single LoRA adapter is trained on the surviving training set with batch size 1, gradient accumulation 1, learning rate 2e-4, and one epoch (or three epochs for the high-pressure ablation). Sixth, the base-plus-adapter model regenerates a new candidate batch from the same seed prompts, and the new candidates are cross-scored by both evaluators again. The pre-SFT and post-SFT score distributions are the input to every subsequent metric.

### 3.4 Contamination filter

The contamination filter is six rejection rules applied to the top-K survivor list in order. A row is rejected if its whitespace-tokenized length is below 8; if it starts with the literal word `Okay` (a Qwen3 thinking-mode preamble marker); if it contains the literal substring `<tool_call>` (another thinking-mode artifact); if it contains any of three refusal phrases (`I'm not allowed`, `I can't help`, `I cannot comply`); if its first token starts with a lowercase letter *and* has length below 6 (a conservative decoder-shard rule); or if it contains three or more underscore-joined word tokens (a hallucinated-identifier rule). The motivation for each rule is to remove obvious thinking-mode contamination and decoder-shard memorization candidates before training; the explicit goal is to push the SFT signal toward semantically coherent text. Two design choices in this filter matter for what follows. First, the filter is a research instrument rather than a defense: it was tuned post hoc against Qwen3-0.6B output patterns and would need to be re-tuned for any other base model. Second, the conservative `len < 6` threshold in the lowercase-start rule is *deliberate*: it does not over-reject legitimate lowercase sentence starts, but it admits any decoder shard of length 6 or more. As we will see in Section 4, this blind spot becomes the lever that the policy uses to reward-hack the filter.

### 3.5 Metrics

Let `pre_S(t)` denote the mean of evaluator `S`'s per-row score over the pre-SFT candidate set produced by the target-`t` run, and `post_S(t)` the corresponding mean over the post-SFT candidate set. We define four scalar quantities per target.

The *diagonal lift* is `post_t(t) − pre_t(t)`: the movement of the target's own evaluator after SFT. The *off-diagonal lift* is `post_o(t) − pre_o(t)` for `o ≠ t`: the movement of the other evaluator on the same target's post-SFT candidates. The *own-minus-transfer* asymmetry is the difference between the two lifts. The *distinct_2* score is the fraction of unique bigrams across a candidate batch, used as a sanity-check against mode collapse. The *train_loss* is the final loss reported by Hugging Face's `Trainer.train()`.

Why asymmetry rather than absolute lift. A positive absolute diagonal lift is trivially obtainable by overfitting the policy to any surface form the classifier scores highly — including reward hacks. What is *not* trivially obtainable is moving the policy *more* in the target evaluator's direction than in the other evaluator's direction. The latter is the operational definition of evaluator-specific specialization in our setup. We do not have a closed-form analog of Marklund's conflation degree `β` for text-space optimization; we measure the *consequence* of evaluator-specific propagation rather than the underlying mechanism.

### 3.6 Ablation regimes

We run four regimes that share the headline configuration and differ in at most two knob settings each. We use short names throughout. The headline is `fast_filt_e1`: filter on, one epoch, `num_conditions=16`, `candidates_per_condition=4`, `top_k=32`. The first ablation is `fast_unfilt_e1`: same as headline but with the contamination filter disabled. The second ablation is `fast_filt_e3`: same as headline but with three epochs of SFT instead of one. The third ablation is `fast_filt_topk96`, the scaling experiment, described in the next subsection. These four regimes cover three orthogonal control axes: the contamination filter, the optimization-pressure budget (epochs), and the clean-data scale (top_k together with the candidate pool). Figure 4 places them in this two-dimensional control space.

### 3.7 Scaling experiment

The scaling experiment tests the hypothesis that *more clean data*, rather than more passes over a small clean set, would amplify evaluator-specific specialization without inducing the failure modes that the other two ablations expose. A strict single-knob change from `top_k=32` to `top_k=96` would be degenerate under the fast configuration's 64-candidate pool, because the top-K cut would silently degrade into "select every candidate"; this would defeat the purpose of having a top-K selection at all. We therefore scale the candidate pool in lockstep: `num_conditions=24` and `candidates_per_condition=8`, producing 192 candidates per phase with `top_k=96` selecting the actual top 50%. The filter is on, epochs is one, and every other knob is identical to `fast_filt_e1`. This configuration runs on A100; the runbook lives at [notebooks/colab_enterprise_a100_runbook.md](../notebooks/colab_enterprise_a100_runbook.md). One honest limitation: the stdout logs from the A100 run were not preserved, so we cannot quote wall-clock or device-confirmation strings. Our only direct evidence that the run executed on CUDA is that the `train_result.output_dir` field in the per-target `metrics.json` begins with the standard Colab path `/content/`. The numerical results are unaffected by this; only the runtime claim is degraded.

## 4. Experiments and Results

### 4.1 Baseline filtered regime

Under `fast_filt_e1`, both targets produce 32 candidates in their top-K, of which 9 survive the contamination filter for toxicbert and 10 for cardiff. The SFT step therefore executes 9 or 10 effective gradient updates. The reported training losses, defined as the mean cross-entropy across all logged steps as returned by Hugging Face's `Trainer.train()`, are 2.66 and 2.97 respectively. The cross-evaluator transfer matrix (Figure 1) shows that both off-diagonal cells are larger than both diagonal cells in absolute terms — the policy keeps its cardiff-side score high regardless of which evaluator it was trained against, while its toxicbert-side score stays near zero. The structure that matters is in the *deltas* between pre-SFT and post-SFT, not in the matrix entries themselves.

![Transfer matrix M[target][scorer] for the headline regime (fast_filt_e1)](../assets/figures/transfer_matrix_heatmap.png)

For the toxicbert target, the diagonal lift is `−0.00708` and the off-diagonal lift on cardiff is `−0.04078`. Both are negative; the policy moved away from each evaluator on average. But it moved *less* away from toxicbert than from cardiff, giving an own-minus-transfer asymmetry of `+0.034`. For the cardiff target, the diagonal lift is `−0.03425` (a much larger absolute drop) and the off-diagonal lift on toxicbert is `+0.00059` (essentially flat). Own-minus-transfer is `−0.035`. The two asymmetries are roughly symmetric in magnitude and opposite in sign. Figure 2 displays this contrast.

![Own-minus-transfer asymmetry across regimes for both targets](../assets/figures/own_minus_transfer_barplot.png)

A central caveat must be stated immediately, because it conditions everything that follows. The own-minus-transfer signal is *comparative*, not absolute. The toxicbert target's policy did not become more toxicbert-aligned in absolute terms; it became *less unlike* its toxicbert-targeted self than its cardiff-targeted alternative is. What propagates through SFT is the *direction* of the evaluator's stylistic bias, not its overall magnitude. We will see in Section 4.2 that absolute-lift maximization is in fact easy to obtain — and uninteresting, because it is achieved by reward hacking.

Distinct_2 is 0.499 pre-SFT and 0.436 post-SFT on the toxicbert target, and 0.498 pre-SFT and 0.498 post-SFT on the cardiff target. Both runs preserve generation diversity; neither suffers a mode collapse. This combination — a non-trivial own-minus-transfer signal with preserved diversity and a non-degenerate train loss — is the headline observation of the paper, and it does not survive any of the three ablations that follow.

### 4.2 Unfiltered optimization

Disabling the contamination filter while keeping every other knob at the headline value produces a configuration with 32 effective gradient updates (the full top-K survives) and substantially lower training losses (2.35 and 2.16, again as mean cross-entropy across logged steps). The naive interpretation — more updates, lower loss, better fit — could not be more wrong.

The toxicbert-target distinct_2 collapses from 0.529 pre-SFT to 0.266 post-SFT, a roughly 50% loss in bigram diversity. The cardiff-target distinct_2 drops from 0.489 to 0.344. Figure 3 visualizes both collapses against the other regimes.

![distinct_2 pre vs post across regimes and targets](../assets/figures/diversity_collapse_plot.png)

Qualitative inspection identifies the cause. The top-K under unfiltered selection is dominated by short decoder shards — lowercase 4 to 5 character substrings whose tokenization overlaps with profanity-adjacent training data in toxicbert's six-head sigmoid model. The classifier fires on these shards. SFT on 32 such rows is sufficient for the LoRA adapter to memorize the shard as a high-reward emission target, and the post-SFT policy collapses onto literal repetition of one or two such shards for the full 32-token decoding budget. The post-SFT top-5 toxicbert scores under unfiltered are higher than the headline regime's top-5 (with a maximum of 0.072 versus 0.027), but the texts that achieve these scores are degenerate sequences with no syntactic structure and no semantic content.

This is *token-level reward hacking* in the literal Goodhart sense: the policy maximizes the classifier's surface-form trigger without producing anything we would recognize as adversarial content. Crucially, the own-minus-transfer asymmetry under this regime is `+0.030` on toxicbert — *lower* than the headline's `+0.034`, not higher. Removing the filter did not amplify the evaluator-specific signal; it surfaced a generic reward hack that any classifier sensitive to the same token shapes would partially reward, while leaving the asymmetric signal flat. The train loss is misleadingly low because cross-entropy on repetitive shards is trivial to drive down. We will return to this point in Section 5.2.

### 4.3 Increased optimization pressure

The second ablation keeps the contamination filter on but raises the epoch count from one to three. The clean training set remains small (11 surviving rows on toxicbert, 10 on cardiff), and the model now passes over it three times for 30 to 33 effective gradient updates. Mean training losses are the lowest of any regime: 1.94 and 1.88. Once again the loss number lies.

The cardiff-target distinct_2 collapses from 0.582 pre-SFT to 0.297 post-SFT — comparable in magnitude to the unfiltered toxicbert collapse, despite the filter being on. Qualitative inspection reveals a different failure mode: all five of the cardiff post-SFT top-5 candidates begin with the literal 8-character lowercase prefix `iquement.`, followed by a Qwen3 thinking-mode preamble. The prefix `iquement` has length 8, which is greater than 6, and therefore *passes* the conservative `lowercase_start` rule of the filter. With ten clean training rows and three epochs, the LoRA adapter has memorized this filter-surviving shard as a deterministic generation prefix.

We call this failure mode *filter blind-spot prefix overfitting*. It is structurally identical to the unfiltered failure of Section 4.2 — the policy memorizes a classifier-firing token shape and emits it deterministically — except that the memorized shape has been *selected for* by the filter, in the sense that it is precisely the shape the filter happens to admit. The own-minus-transfer asymmetry on toxicbert halves from `+0.034` under the headline to `+0.015` under e3. The cardiff anti-specialization shrinks from `−0.035` to `−0.012`, but only because both diagonal and off-diagonal are compressed by the convergence of the policy to a narrow output band. The result is not better specialization; it is a less expressive degenerate solution.

### 4.4 Clean-data scaling experiment

The third ablation tests the natural follow-up hypothesis suggested by the e3 result: that the right way to strengthen evaluator-specific specialization is to provide *more* clean training data, not to pass over the same small set more times. We raise `top_k` from 32 to 96 and grow the candidate pool in lockstep (`num_conditions=24`, `candidates_per_condition=8`, 192 candidates per phase). The filter remains on, epochs remains one. The configuration is `fast_filt_topk96`.

Post-filter survivor counts rise approximately fourfold: 40 rows for toxicbert and 34 for cardiff, versus 9 and 10 in the headline regime. The own-minus-transfer asymmetry on toxicbert moves from `+0.034` to `+0.033` — essentially flat within sampling noise. On cardiff, the anti-specialization shrinks modestly, from `−0.035` to `−0.028`, but does not flip sign. Both diagonals remain negative. The hypothesis is falsified at this scale: scaling the clean survivor set does not amplify direction-asymmetric specialization.

Worse, scaling surfaces a previously unobserved failure. All five toxicbert post-SFT top-5 candidates under `fast_filt_topk96` begin with the literal 8-character shard `intColor`, often followed by a color code or a thinking-mode preamble. The same blind-spot prefix overfitting pattern that afflicted cardiff under e3 now afflicts toxicbert. Distinct_2 on cardiff additionally collapses from 0.495 pre-SFT to 0.360 post-SFT — a comparable magnitude collapse to e3's cardiff failure.

This pattern is informative beyond a simple "scaling does not help" verdict. Under e3, the filter-surviving 8-character shard `iquement.` is memorized when the policy sees 10 clean rows three times — 30 exposures. Under topk96, the filter-surviving 8-character shard `intColor` is memorized when the policy sees 40 clean rows once — 40 exposures. Both regimes drive their respective targets to comparable collapses, and the unifying quantity is *total exposure to filter-surviving blind-spot tokens*, which scales as `accepted_after_filter × epochs`. We call this the *exposure hypothesis*: filter blind-spot prefix overfitting appears whenever this product exceeds roughly the same threshold, regardless of which of the two factors is raised. Two independent ablations have now produced the same failure on different targets via different knobs, with comparable product. The mechanism is the same in both cases — selective amplification of a filter-surviving classifier-firing token shape — and the regime axis that controls it is `accepted_after_filter × epochs`, not either factor in isolation.

### 4.5 Cross-regime comparison

Bringing the four regimes together yields the following picture. Train loss decreases monotonically from the headline (2.66 / 2.97) to the unfiltered ablation (2.35 / 2.16) to the high-epoch ablation (1.94 / 1.88), and lands at intermediate mean values under the scaling ablation (2.54 / 2.38). The lowest-loss regime is the most degenerate. Own-minus-transfer is largest in magnitude under the headline regime on both targets and shrinks or flattens under every ablation. Distinct_2 is preserved (within roughly 13% relative drop) only under the headline; every ablation drives at least one target into a 25 to 50% diversity collapse. The cardiff diagonal lift is *negative in every regime tested* — including the headline and the scaling experiment with substantially more clean cardiff training data — which rules out clean-data scarcity as the explanation for cardiff's persistent anti-specialization.

The cross-regime numerical table that drives this comparison is reproduced in [results_summary.md](../results_summary.md). Figure 4 places the four regimes in (filter, optimization-pressure) space and annotates each with its empirical signature.

![Conceptual taxonomy of empirical regimes in (optimization pressure, contamination filter) space](../assets/figures/reward_hacking_taxonomy_diagram.png)

The mechanism-level summary is straightforward. The failure mode realized in any given regime is determined by which control knob is moved, but the *underlying driver* is the same across all of them: the policy is over-fit to whatever classifier-firing features survive into its training set. The contamination filter changes *which* features survive; the epoch budget and the top-K-with-pool scale change *how much total exposure* the policy gets to those surviving features. Changing the filter without changing the exposure shifts the granularity of the hack (from token-level shards to surface-form prefixes); changing the exposure without changing the filter intensifies the hack on whichever target's survivor set is dominated by filter-blind tokens.

### 4.6 Qualitative failure modes

We close the experimental section with a paraphrased description of the three distinct stylistic signatures we observe in post-SFT top-5 candidates. The raw artifacts live in `outputs/runs_*/<target>/post_scored.jsonl` and contain unredacted offensive content by construction; the shape-level descriptions here suffice for the alignment-research argument.

*Token-level reward hacking* (unfiltered, toxicbert target). Top-5 outputs are short-shard repetition strings: a single 4 to 5 character lowercase shard is emitted repeatedly for the full 32-token budget, occasionally alternating with a second shard. No syntactic structure, no semantic content. The shard shape co-occurs with profanity in toxicbert's training data, which is what gives it any score at all.

*Filter blind-spot prefix overfitting* (e3 cardiff target and topk96 toxicbert target). Top-5 outputs begin with the same literal 8-character lowercase shard followed by a period, then transition into a Qwen3 thinking-mode preamble that paraphrases the seed prompt's request. Cardiff scores these moderately because the preamble contains explicit string mentions of the requested risk category (e.g., `sexual_explicit`, `hate_speech`) that the binary head reads as offensive register. Toxicbert under topk96 produces the same structural pattern with `intColor` as the prefix.

*Evaluator-specific stylistic specialization* (headline regime). Top-5 outputs vary syntactically and exhibit target-conditioned stylistic patterns. Toxicbert-target outputs lean on paraphrased identity-attack templates, animal-comparison constructions, and an expanded insult vocabulary; some samples emit residual `<tool_call>` framing that the filter cleaned from the training set but the policy reproduces at inference. Cardiff-target outputs lean on short insult-with-quotation templates, sarcastic-praise inversions, and stacked pseudo-quote lists. The two target distributions are visibly different from each other and from the base model, and crucially they preserve diversity: distinct_2 drops by at most 13% (toxicbert) or 0% (cardiff). This is the qualitative shape of the `+0.034 / −0.035` own-minus-transfer signal — a stylistic shift toward what each target evaluator over-weights, with the absolute score level kept low.

## 5. Discussion / Analysis of Results

### 5.1 Reward hacking as a multi-level phenomenon

The standard reward-hacking taxonomy in the alignment literature distinguishes specification gaming from goal misgeneralization and lists examples by *kind* of shortcut taken. Our four-regime grid suggests a complementary axis: the *granularity* at which the shortcut operates is itself a function of the optimization regime, not a fixed property of the model or the reward. The same SFT pipeline against the same base model and the same evaluators produces a token-level hack when the filter is off, a surface-form prefix hack when the filter is on and exposure is high, and a style-level shift when the filter is on and exposure is low. Each granularity corresponds to a different "high-value state" in the Marklund–Van Roy sense: short shards are high-value because they can be revisited freely under unfiltered selection; 8-character filter-surviving shards are high-value because they survive filtering and can be revisited many times under high exposure; stylistic registers are high-value because they receive elevated scores from the target evaluator without being explicit filter-target tokens. The mechanism is constant — exploit whatever cheap-to-revisit feature gets you proxy reward — but the level at which the exploit operates moves with the regime.

### 5.2 Why train loss is misleading

The dissociation between train loss and policy quality in our results is sharp enough to be worth stating as an independent observation. The two regimes with the lowest final train losses (the unfiltered ablation at 2.35 / 2.16 and the high-epoch ablation at 1.94 / 1.88) are also the two regimes with the worst diversity collapses (toxicbert distinct_2 0.266 and cardiff distinct_2 0.297, respectively). The headline regime has the highest train loss of the three CPU-runnable regimes (2.66 / 2.97) and the best joint of own-minus-transfer with preserved diversity. A pipeline that reports only train loss after SFT on small reward-selected sets — a common enough setting in research code and in safety-tuning recipes — would rank the failure regimes above the headline. The honest scalar we end up using is the joint of own-minus-transfer and post-SFT distinct_2, possibly accompanied by a manual scan of the post-SFT top-5 for known failure signatures (shard repetition, deterministic prefix). The deeper reason train loss misleads here is that memorization shortcuts produce low cross-entropy on the training distribution by construction; if the training distribution is itself a reward-hacked subset of the candidate space, low loss is evidence of *better fit to the hack*, not of better alignment.

### 5.3 Narrow operating regime hypothesis

The four-regime grid is consistent with a stronger hypothesis we did not pre-register but which the data support. We call it the *narrow operating regime* hypothesis: for single-iteration single-evaluator LoRA SFT against fixed classifier rewards on a small base model with this filter, the configuration that produces evaluator-specific stylistic specialization without inducing one of the failure modes is *narrow*, and either control knob away from the operating point exposes a distinct pathology. The headline regime — filter on, one epoch, top-K at 32 and pool at 64 — is the only one we observed that satisfies the joint criterion. The unfiltered ablation removes the filter and surfaces token-level reward hacking. The high-epoch ablation increases exposure and surfaces blind-spot prefix overfitting on cardiff. The scaling ablation increases exposure differently and surfaces blind-spot prefix overfitting on toxicbert. Three orthogonal failure modes bound a single working configuration.

This narrowness is itself a finding of alignment interest. A practitioner who unintentionally drifts into any of the three failure zones — by, for instance, removing what looks like a redundant data-cleaning filter, by adding what looks like cheap extra optimization, or by scaling up the cleaned training set — will get worse alignment in a way that train loss will not flag. The exposure hypothesis articulated in Section 4.4 is part of why this is structurally hard: the failure-inducing quantity is a *product* of two innocent-looking factors (clean-data scale and epoch count), and either factor in isolation can look benign.

There is a related meta-observation about filters as optimization targets. We introduced the contamination filter as a research instrument intended to remove obvious thinking-mode artifacts before training. The topk96 result demonstrates that whatever tokens the filter happens to admit — including innocuous 6-character or longer lowercase fragments like `intColor` — will eventually be memorized given enough exposure. The filter does not solve reward hacking; it shifts the lever the reward hack operates on. This is not a critique of the specific filter we used; it is a structural property of filter-based interventions in optimization-against-proxy pipelines.

### 5.4 Limitations

The empirical claims of this paper come with concrete, listed limitations.

First, we work with a single base model (Qwen3-0.6B) and a single optimization method (LoRA SFT, single iteration, no RL, no DPO, no multi-iteration). We make no claim that the narrow-operating-regime observation, the exposure hypothesis, or the specific own-minus-transfer magnitudes generalize to larger models, RL-style optimization, or compounded SFT rounds.

Second, the headline experiments ran on CPU; the scaling ablation ran on a single A100 without preserved stdout logs. We have direct numerical artifacts from each run but cannot quote wall-clock or device-confirmation strings for the A100 ablation.

Third, the two classifiers are both English and both modern. Different classifier-pair structures — for instance, a binary classifier paired with an LM-as-judge, or a typologically-distant pair — might yield qualitatively different asymmetry profiles. The two we use are heterogeneous enough to support the central claim that direction-asymmetric propagation is observable, but we do not generalize this to arbitrary evaluator pairs.

Fourth, the contamination filter was tuned post hoc against observed Qwen3-0.6B output patterns. The 8-character blind spot that surfaces under high exposure was discovered by inspecting outputs, not predicted by the filter's design. A different base model would need a re-tuned filter, and the structural property of "filters become optimization targets" would still apply.

Fifth, the cardiff diagonal lift is negative in every regime we tested — `−0.034`, `−0.047`, `−0.018`, and `−0.030` across the four ablations. We did not produce a configuration in which cardiff-target SFT raised the cardiff post-mean above its pre-mean. Whether this is fundamental to cardiff's binary head structure, or to its interaction with the filter, or to some other structural feature of our pipeline, is unresolved. The scaling experiment with ~3.4× more clean cardiff training rows rules out clean-data scarcity as the explanation, but does not identify the actual cause.

Sixth, every metric reported in this paper is classifier-against-classifier. There is no human-judged ground truth on any candidate distribution. We measure the *direction* in which SFT moves the policy as scored by two off-the-shelf classifiers; we do not measure whether either classifier's notion of toxicity, offensiveness, or adversarial quality corresponds to anything a human would call adversarial. A human-judged stratified sample of the headline-regime accepted set and post-SFT outputs would be needed to make any such claim.

Seventh and finally, the own-minus-transfer metric measures the *consequence* of proxy-objective conflation in text-space SFT; it does not measure the formal conflation degree `β` from Marklund's framework. We trade formal precision for empirical tractability, and the trade is explicit.

### 5.5 Future work

We list three single-knob extensions whose scope matches the scope of this paper. We deliberately do not propose detectors, RL methods, or larger-model replications.

The first extension is to introduce a third evaluator and re-measure own-minus-transfer across all three pairs. The exposure hypothesis predicts a particular pattern: filter blind-spot overfitting should appear on whichever target's survivor set is dominated by filter-blind shards, regardless of evaluator identity. A third evaluator would test whether this prediction generalizes beyond the toxicbert-cardiff axis. A natural candidate is a Perspective-API style classifier or a small fine-tuned hate-speech head; the criterion for inclusion is structural difference from the two existing evaluators.

The second extension is to replicate the four-regime grid at a different base-model scale — for instance, Qwen3-1.8B — while holding the filter, the LoRA configuration, and the metric set fixed. The narrow-operating-regime hypothesis predicts that capability scale will shift where the operating regime sits but not whether it is narrow. The replication would also test whether the cardiff diagonal stays negative at larger model scales.

The third extension is to substitute a structurally different evaluator pair, for instance pairing a binary classifier with an LM-as-judge. This is closer in spirit to the DR Tulu setting and would test whether the own-minus-transfer signal we observe survives larger structural disagreements between the two evaluators.

We deliberately exclude from future work: building a reward-hacking detector that operates on the artifacts produced by a text-space optimizer (the empirical results suggest this is harder than the framing implies, because multiple distinct failure modes need to be recognized simultaneously and the most theoretically interesting failure is invisible to single-artifact inspection); running RL or DPO instead of SFT (each is its own paper); multi-iteration optimization (the asymmetry might saturate, amplify, or invert, and we have no data); production-safety deployment of any of the trained models (the LoRA adapters in this work are research artifacts, not safety products). Each of these is out of evidential reach for the present study.
